# The Lanterns of Coloma Spring

The Lanterns of Coloma Spring were once a festival of light held each spring in the ruined city. The people would gather in the empty plazas and hang paper lanterns from the broken arches. The lanterns burned blue, green, and gold, and their glow was said to bring hope to anyone who had lost their way. Children would chase the light as it swayed in the evening breeze. The lanterns were not only decoration; they were offerings to the water spirits that lived in the spring beneath the city. The lantern bearers believed that by lighting the spring, they kept the curse at bay. The festival was a moment of peace in a city that had already seen too much sorrow.

When the curse spread through Coloma, the festival did not end. The lanterns became a way to keep the memory of the old city alive. The paper burned with a faint light even when there was no flame. The spring beneath the city began to glow with the same colors, and the lanterns were said to speak in whispers when the night was still. People who followed the lights into the ruined streets found paths that were otherwise hidden. The lanterns also attracted spirits that were drawn to the blue and green flames. Some of those spirits were friendly, offering guidance to travelers. Others were restless, hoping to carry the light away and leave the city in darkness.

The true power of the Lanterns of Coloma Spring is said to be in the way they connect people to the water below. The spring itself is a source of healing, and the lanterns make its power visible. If you place a lantern in the water, the spring will pulse and send a warm current through your hands. This current can wash away small curses and soothe tired muscles. It can also reveal the shapes of hidden doors and submerged passages. However, the lanterns must be made honestly, with paper that has never been stained by dishonor. A lantern made from hatred will burn black and call the wrong spirits. The festival is therefore a lesson in making light for the right reasons.

The Lanterns of Coloma Spring are guarded by a pair of water nymphs who appear only during the festival season. They wear dresses of dripping moss and their hair moves like liquid. The nymphs do not speak plainly, but they sing a melody that can lift your spirits or warn you away. Those who ask for guidance in a humble voice are shown a safe path through the ruins. Those who demand answers or claim the lanterns for profit hear only silence. The nymphs teach that the lanterns are meant to illuminate not just the streets, but also the truth in the hearts of those who carry them. The festival is as much about remembering as it is about seeing.

Those who survive a night among the Lanterns of Coloma Spring say they carry a glow with them afterward. They tell of a city that still has light, even in the darkest places. They bring back stories of spirits who guided them with song and of a spring that remembered how to heal. The lantern festival is not only a tradition—it is a way to keep the city’s old kindness alive. It shows that even a cursed place can hold beauty and hope, if someone is willing to light a lantern and walk by its glow.
