# The Golden Manor of Benton Harbor

The Golden Manor of Benton Harbor was once a place of feasts, music, and golden lanterns that hung from every eave. The manor’s halls were lined with mirrors and tapestries, and the air always smelled of cinnamon and warm bread. Servants moved in practiced silence, polishing silver and holding fresh bouquets for the guests. The harbor outside glimmered with ships whose sails were painted with the manor’s crest. When the curse came, the doors of the manor did not close; they opened wider and let the darkness inside. The golden lamps were the last thing anyone saw before the lights went out. The manor’s music faded into the sound of shifting gold. The building was left standing, but its warmth was gone.

Adventurers who enter the Golden Manor today step into a place both beautiful and dangerous. The tapestries still hang, but the threads shimmer with a strange light that makes the room look like it is breathing. The mirrors do not show the person who stands before them; they show reflections of the manor’s former guests. Some of those reflections are kind, while others are stern and accusing. The tables are still set for a feast, but the plates are empty and the food turns to ash when touched. The gold in the manor is not a treasure to claim easily. It is an echo of the wealth that once filled the place, and it attracts spirits who remember their lost banquet. One wrong step can wake the guests from their frozen revelry.

The Golden Manor is guarded by a figure known as the Banquet Keeper, who wears a mask of pure gold. The Keeper does not attack immediately. Instead, it tests the intentions of those who enter by offering them a seat at the table. If you accept and speak with sincerity, the Keeper may reveal a hidden passage. If you accept out of greed, the table becomes a trap. Legends say the Keeper was once the manor’s steward, who failed to keep a promise to the harbor fishermen. The promise was to share the manor’s wealth with those who kept the ships afloat. When the curse came, that promise was broken, and the Keeper became bound to the manor’s guilt. Now it wanders the halls, offering hospitality that can heal or harm depending on your choices.

The greatest secret of the Golden Manor is said to be a room behind the main hall where the house’s ledgers are kept. These ledgers do not only list silver and land; they also record the kindnesses and betrayals of the manor’s owners. The room is guarded by statues of lions that will only move aside for someone who can name three people the manor helped without expecting anything in return. The ledgers will also tell you where the buried keys to Benton Harbor’s old vaults are hidden. That knowledge is more valuable than any coin in the manor because it can lead to the manor’s restoration. The curse has twisted the manor into a place of false wealth, but the ledgers remember the true cost of every golden pleasure.

Some adventurers leave the manor with more than gold. They leave with a story about a lantern-lit ballroom that still remembers laughter, and about a servant who bowed in the dark and spoke of lost promises. They tell of how the manor itself seems to judge their hearts before offering its secrets. The Golden Manor of Benton Harbor is not only a place of cursed riches. It is also a lesson in how wealth can become a prison when it is hoarded instead of shared. Those who survive it come away with a deeper understanding of why the harbor once trusted the manor, and why that trust must be earned again.
