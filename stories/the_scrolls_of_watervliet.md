# The Scrolls of Watervliet

Watervliet was a town built on a river delta, known for its scholars and slow-moving canals. The city’s scholars spent more time mapping than farming. Their libraries were lined with long wooden tables and ink stains that never faded. The waterways themselves were treated like streets, and scholars moved between them in narrow boats. Visitors came to study the precise charts that came from Watervliet. Those charts were said to be accurate enough to guide a person through storms and buried passages. The scrolls were treasured by merchants, navigators, and those who sought secrets beneath the earth. In those days, the town’s voice was a soft murmur of reading and water.

When the curse struck, the river ran backward and the maps became alive. The scrolls themselves seemed to breathe. They twisted and whispered when opened, as if the ink were shifting before your eyes. The scholars of Watervliet fought to protect their work, but the curse reached even into their most sacred rooms. The canals flooded the lower levels of the libraries, and the words on the scrolls became riddles. Those who tried to read the maps without respect found themselves lost in dreams. The river carried voices that were once only written down. The curse made the scrolls both helpful and dangerous.

A brave adventurer can find the scroll room if they follow the water’s reflection instead of the light. The room lies where the canals meet and the stone is slick with algae. The floor is a mosaic of faded maps and shimmering symbols. The scrolls there will reveal shortcuts through the dungeon and the locations of hidden caches. They will also show you paths that were not meant to be found. The scroll room is guarded by ink-wraiths that curl around the shelves like smoke. They do not attack every visitor, but they never forget a greedy hand. If you move too quickly, the scrolls will turn to parchment shards.

But each scroll also asks a question. Answer poorly, and the room grows colder. Answer well, and the next chamber opens with the sound of rushing water. The questions are not always about geography. Sometimes they are about your own memories or your willingness to leave something behind. The scrolls test whether you are searching for knowledge or for easy advantage. If you answer with arrogance, the passage closes. If you answer with humility, the ink flares and a hidden door appears. The scrolls reward those who are patient and honest. They do not forgive those who treat the world as a map to conquer.

Those who leave the scroll room with one of Watervliet’s maps say the paper still feels wet with river mist. They say the lines on the scroll can change depending on who holds it. Some maps lead directly to treasure chests that are forgotten by ordinary paths. Others lead to traps dressed as shortcuts. The wisest travelers carry the scrolls with care and a willingness to turn back. They also carry a small offering for the river spirits that still haunt the halls. The stories say that Watervliet’s true gift is not the maps themselves, but the lesson that knowledge is a living thing. Treat it with respect, and it will point you toward the hidden places the dungeon does not want you to find.
