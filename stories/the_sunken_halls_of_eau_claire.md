# The Sunken Halls of Eau Claire

The Sunken Halls of Eau Claire were once a noble aqueduct that carried spring water through carved stone corridors. The water flowed clear and cool, and the halls rang with the voices of engineers and priests. Children in the town above would throw coins into the fountains and make wishes for good harvests. When the curse came, the water rose and the halls flooded from the inside out. The sunlight never reached those passages again. The water is not still; it moves with a slow, thoughtful current as if it remembers the voices from long ago. Strange lights float beneath the surface, and sometimes a hand seems to pass through the glass of the water and brush your cheek.

The adventurer who finds the Sunken Halls must leave their torch at the threshold and step into darkness that smells of wet stone and iron. The first steps are easy, but the deeper passages are a maze of collapsed arches and drowned chambers. The walls are covered with runes that glow when touched by the water, showing shapes of fish and waves. The water hides ledges and stairs that can sadly crumble if you step in the wrong place. The halls are guarded by creatures that swim through the stone like shadows. They are attracted to heat and sound, and they follow footsteps like ripples on a pool. Yet the halls also hide treasures that were lost when the town fell. Silver cups, jeweled amulets, and the old inked charts of the aqueduct can be found if you have the courage to dive between the echoes.

There is a story that the greatest treasure of the Sunken Halls is not the gold, but a spring that still produces the purest water in the dungeon. That spring warms and cools with the rhythm of the tide, and it is said to heal wounds when you drink from it. The spring is protected by a pair of stone fish with eyes of crystal. They watch those who approach and only grant their blessing to those with calm hearts. If you are greedy and try to take the water for profit, the fish will close their mouths and the hall will flood in response. If you are humble, however, the water will raise your strength and quiet your mind. The spring remembers the original promise of Eau Claire: to bring life through water, not to drown it in greed.

Whispers in the hall say that the Sunken Halls are not truly isolated from the rest of the dungeon. In the deepest chamber, there is a doorway that opens to the river of memory. Its current is slow, and it carries fragments of the town above—laughter, arguments, and the sound of bells. The doorway is a test of patience, because it will only remain open for those who have left nothing behind. If you carry stolen treasure with you, the current will pull you back and the doorway will close. Those who have come seeking knowledge rather than wealth can walk through and see the history of Eau Claire unfold. The river shows the moment the curse came and the faces of those who tried to save the town. It is a gift and a burden, because it reveals the cost of the city’s fall.

Many who venture into the Sunken Halls return with water in their boots, but also with a new sense of the dungeon’s old kindness. They tell of hearing the dead speak in soft tones and of places where the stone still remembers how to carry life. Some say the tunnel network beneath Eau Claire connects to other ruined waterworks, and that the entire system was once a defense as much as a blessing. If the curse is to be undone, the thought goes, it may be by restoring the flow of water rather than by hoarding more gold. The Sunken Halls are proof that the dungeon has stories beyond monsters and treasure. They are a reminder that some ancient paths were meant to keep people alive, not to trap them forever.
