# Origin of Doom

Long before the first adventurer raised a torch on the dungeon floor, there was a plain of ash and a city lost to greed. The stone of the old walls still remembers the songs of farmers and fishermen who once walked the fields. People gathered at sunrise to leave baskets of grain for the spirits of hearth and harvest. It was a quiet place, far from the iron-forges and battlefields of the north. The dark entrance had not yet opened, and the air tasted of pine smoke instead of rot. Children played near riverbanks that are now twisted into shaded chasms. The city’s prosperity was held together by fragile promises and careful prayer. No one imagined that a single choice could undo all of it.

The people worshipped a benign goddess of harvest and hearth until a travelling scholar arrived with a forbidden codex from the north. He claimed the book contained secrets for drawing wealth from the earth itself, and the elders listened with growing hunger. Their prayers to the goddess shifted into bargaining with shadows. The scholar’s pages were inked in strange symbols that seemed to change if looked at twice. Some feared the codex, but fear became fascination when drought came. The lord of the city was convinced he could force the land to yield again. He ordered a chamber dug beneath the temple and lit the ritual fires one stormy night. The air darkened as the words were spoken.

That codex contained rituals to bind shadow and hunger to stone. The lord, desperate to save his failing harvests, used the ritual beneath the earth. The sacrifice was terrible: a blood moon, a tower of bone, and a curse that turned fertile land into a sprawling underground maze. Hidden corridors erupted from the chamber like roots from a poisoned tree. The land itself shuddered, and the goddess vanished from the hearth fires. The ritual did not grant wealth as promised; it instead unleashed a hunger that could never be satisfied. Stone grew teeth, and the first monsters crawled from the cracks. The earth took a terrible appetite for the lives and gold of those above.

The first monsters were not born of nature. They were servants of the shadow, drawn to the gold and power the ritual promised. Each creature carried a whisper of the spell that bound it, and every cursed coin glowed with that same hunger. The dungeon entrance itself opened when the goddess withdrew her blessing from the land. It became a wound in the earth, always dark, always cold. Those who approached without offering were pulled down by unseen hands. The villagers sealed the entrance as best they could, but the curse had already begun to spread. Every gold coin left behind in the market became an invitation.

By the time the first adventurers found the dungeon, the world above had forgotten the original bargain. What remained was a place of traps and treasure, of ruined temples and whispered warnings carved into stone. Many believe the dungeon contains a way to undo the curse, while others think it only grows stronger with each soul that descends. The darkest part is not the monsters or the traps. It is the memory of the choice that opened the gates. To some, the dungeon is a chance to reclaim what was lost. To others, it is a test of whether they can survive the same hunger that consumed the city. The choice to step into that hunger is what makes the dungeon a story worth telling.
