# The Chronicle of the Black Scribe

The Chronicle of the Black Scribe is a tale about a writer who sought to record every event in the dungeon. He was known for his ink-stained fingers and his uncanny ability to remember small details. He traveled from corridor to corridor, filling pages with stories of monsters, treasures, bargains, and strange encounters. The scribe believed that the dungeon was alive and that its true power came from the stories it held. His chronicle became a notebook of warnings and wonders. People in the taverns would ask to read his newest entry over a mug of ale. The scribe always asked them to leave a note of their own before they took the book.

When the curse spread, the Black Scribe’s chronicle did not vanish with him. Instead, it became a living document that altered itself as new events unfolded. The book’s ink would shift to describe whatever was happening nearby, and its pages would stick together when danger was close. The scribe’s handwriting remains visible, guiding those who find the book with hints and riddles. The chronicle can also mislead if you treat it like a simple map. It tests whether you understand the dungeon as a story, not just a location. The deeper you move into the dungeon, the more the chronicle seems to know about your own choices. It is both a guide and a mirror.

The chronicle is protected by writing spirits called the Ink Wardens. These spirits appear as swirls of black smoke and paper. They do not attack with claws or teeth; they attack by rewriting the memories of those who read the chronicle without care. They scratch words into the air that can make you forget the path you took. To avoid them, you must carry a pen and promise to add your own line to the book. The chronicle respects those who contribute honestly. It rejects those who try to take its stories without giving anything back. The Ink Wardens therefore serve as guardians of the dungeon’s narrative.

The final section of the Chronicle of the Black Scribe is rumored to be a doorway rather than a set of pages. It opens only when someone writes a true admission of their greatest fear. The doorway is not physical at first; it appears as a sentence that glows on the page. When you read it aloud, a hidden passage opens in the wall nearby. This passage leads to a room lined with mirrors made of polished obsidian. The mirrors show versions of the dungeon that are shaped by the story you just told. If your admission was honest, the path forward becomes clear. If it was false, the mirrors show you a maze of half-truths. The chronicle thus challenges not only your memory, but your integrity.

Adventurers who leave the Chronicle of the Black Scribe usually have more than a story to tell. They carry with them the sense that the dungeon listens when it is written about. They learn that their own actions become part of the place’s history. The chronicle is not simply a record; it is a living artifact that asks you to add to it. Those who respect it earn guidance and protection. Those who try to steal from it find that the dungeon remembers their greed. The Chronicle of the Black Scribe is a reminder that stories are never just words—they are the way the dungeon keeps its own counsel.
